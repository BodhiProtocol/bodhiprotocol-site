Most people think Meta sells social media. It doesn't, not really — the posts, the friends, the messages were always free.

Meta's real product isn't the feed. It's the number of seconds it can hold your attention on it. Everything else supports that objective.

## The Invisible Business

Imagine opening the app to check one notification and looking up forty minutes later. You didn't plan to stay. Something kept adjusting to keep you there.

That pull isn't accidental. Behind it sits one invisible question: what's the next post that keeps this person scrolling for three more seconds? That's the business.

An algorithm that learns from every pause, like, and rewatch. A feed that never runs out. Notifications timed to interrupt at the moment you're most likely to return. Every layer points toward a single outcome: attention, captured continuously and sold by the second.

## Why The Feed Isn't The Product

Meta spends enormous effort improving its recommendation algorithm. Most people assume better content is the business.

Better content is actually the mechanism, not the business. The real business is converting your attention into a prediction precise enough that an advertiser will pay for it — not a demographic guess, but a read on what you're actually likely to do next.

Viewed that way, every Meta decision suddenly makes sense: why the feed has no natural end, why engagement, not satisfaction, is what gets optimized, why the app knows what you want before you've searched for it.
