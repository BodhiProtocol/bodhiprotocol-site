Twice a year, Bill Gates disappears. No phone, no family, no staff — just a cabin on the Washington coast and a locked case of paper: hundreds of memos, unpublished research, and submissions from his own employees, stacked by the foot. By the end of the week, most of it will never be mentioned again. A handful of ideas will leave that cabin and reshape a company, or redirect a decade of a foundation's spending.

That week is the real subject of this page — not the products or the diseases it eventually pointed at, but the discipline underneath both: read enormously, then commit to almost none of it. The same funnel that once narrowed a flood of engineering memos down to "own the operating system, not the hardware" later narrowed a flood of public-health research down to "fund malaria before it's fashionable." Different inputs, same shape of mind.
