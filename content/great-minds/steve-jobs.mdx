In December 1979, Steve Jobs walked into a research lab at Xerox PARC and watched a demonstration that lasted perhaps ten minutes. A researcher moved a small device across a desk, and a pointer moved with it on a screen full of overlapping windows and little pictures instead of typed commands. Jobs would later say he was so excited he could barely speak. Within days, he had reorganized part of Apple's engineering team around building a machine that could do the same thing — not for researchers, but for anyone.

That moment is the real subject of this page, more than any single product that followed. Jobs didn't invent the graphical interface, the mouse, or the personal computer. What he had, again and again, was the instinct to recognize something built for a narrow, technical purpose and see, immediately, what it would take to hand it to an ordinary person — and then the discipline to cut away everything standing in the way.
