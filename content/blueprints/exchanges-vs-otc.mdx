**Core question:** where do trades actually happen, and why does the venue change what the trade means?

## The metaphor: a farmer's market vs. a private phone call

An exchange is a farmer's market: every buyer and seller sees the same posted prices at the same stall, and the trade happens where anyone standing nearby could have watched it. **OTC** — over-the-counter — is a private phone call between two parties instead. The deal terms, and often the price itself, are known only to the two people who made it. Same goods can move through either channel. The venue decides who else gets to know what happened.

<Callout type="insight" title="Public price vs. private deal">
An exchange trade creates a public price everyone can reference for the next trade. An OTC trade doesn't — which is exactly why complex, bespoke instruments (many swaps, structured products) trade OTC in the first place. No public stall would fit a deal this custom.
</Callout>

## Same asset, different venue, different price

The exact same stock can move through both channels. Most shares trade on the exchange, at the visible posted price. But a large institutional block of that same stock might get negotiated privately instead — specifically to avoid moving the public price before the trade is even finished. Size and privacy, not the asset itself, often decide which venue a trade uses.

<Callout type="question">
Next time you see a price quoted, ask: was this posted on an exchange, where anyone could have gotten the same number — or was it negotiated OTC, between two specific parties who agreed to it privately?
</Callout>

## Why this matters

The venue isn't a technical detail — it's the difference between a price the whole market can react to and a price only two people will ever see. Regulation, liquidity, and even how confidently you can trust a quoted number all trace back to which of these two a trade actually went through.
