**Core question:** why does the first number you hear distort every estimate that follows, even when that number is irrelevant?

## The metaphor: a boat's anchor dropped in the wrong spot

Once a boat drops anchor, it can still drift — but only within the length of the chain from that spot. The first number you hear works the same way on a judgment: your estimate can still move from there, but it drifts within a radius set by that first number, even when the first number was arbitrary, wrong, or chosen specifically to mislead you.

<Callout type="insight" title="It works even when you know it's meaningless">
In the classic experiment, people spin a wheel that lands on a random number, then get asked an unrelated factual question. Their estimates correlate with the random number they just saw — even though every one of them knew, going in, that the wheel was random and had nothing to do with the question.
</Callout>

## Negotiators use this on purpose

The first number stated in a negotiation is rarely a serious opening offer — it's an anchor placement. A deliberately extreme number, said first, is meant to drag the final agreed number toward it, because whoever responds is usually countering *from that number* instead of from their own independent estimate. The anchor doesn't need to be believed to work. It just needs to be heard first.

<Callout type="question">
Before responding to an opening number in any negotiation, ask: what would I have estimated if I'd never heard this number at all? Answer that silently, before reacting to theirs.
</Callout>

## Why this matters

Knowing about anchoring doesn't make you immune to it — the wheel experiment worked on people who understood probability perfectly well. The only real defense is forming your own independent estimate *before* hearing someone else's number, because once you've heard it, your judgment is already anchored, whether you meant it to be or not.
